# Library of Varrock

The Library of Varrock in the original RuneScape was a place of lore — books, letters, and stories that added depth to the world beyond its combat and skills. This section serves the same purpose for the 2004SP project.

## The Story of the Project

Lost City Singleplayer Progressive began with a simple question: what if the bots that used to fill RuneScape servers in 2004 were real progressors — intelligent, purposeful, living out a genuine RuneScape life rather than just spam-clicking one spot?

The answer required rebuilding the entire bot AI from scratch on top of the Lost City 2004 engine. Every skill needed a progression path. Every skill needed banking logic. Every bot needed to understand the world — gates, tolls, boats, quest requirements, combat levels, prayer points.

## Historical Roots — RS2 Progressive by mige

This project is a spiritual successor to **EasyRS2 / RS2 Progressive**, originally created by **mige**. That project was one of the earliest attempts at a progressive single-player RS2 experience, proving the concept long before modern tooling made it straightforward.

The mige launcher and client modification from that era are preserved for historical reference via the Archive.org and MEGA mirror links on the main page. Both mirrors are kept available so the original work remains accessible as links and communities change over time.

## Letters from the Wilderness

*The following is lore written in the style of RuneScape's in-game letters, set in the world of this server.*

**To whoever finds this parchment,**

I have been watching the travellers pass through Lumbridge for three weeks now. They fish at the same spots each morning, chop the same trees each afternoon, and bank at Varrock by nightfall. They never speak, but they are purposeful — more purposeful than most players I have encountered.

I do not know if they are aware of me. I fought one near the Chaos Altar. It used Protect from Melee without hesitation and switched to a dragon dagger special with alarming timing.

I suspect I am not alone in this world. I suspect none of us are.

— A traveller, written at Edgeville bank, Year Unknown
